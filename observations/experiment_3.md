# Experiment 3 — Food Scarcity Environment

## Configuration

**Modified Parameter**

food_spawn_rate = 0.7

**Previous Value**

food_spawn_rate = 1.2

**Observation Duration**

Approximately 20 minutes

---

# Objective

To observe how reducing food availability affects:

- Population size
- Species diversity
- Evolutionary stability
- Movement speed
- Clustering and territorial behavior
- Long-term survival of species

---

# Observations

## 1. Rapid Species Turnover

A repeating sequence of dominant species was observed:

Yellow
↓
Orange
↓
Red
↓
Grey
↓
Purple


Many species briefly became dominant, expanded rapidly, and then disappeared.

No single newly emerged species maintained dominance for a long period.

---

## 2. Boom-and-Bust Population Cycles

A recurring pattern was observed:

Species emerges
↓
Population explosion
↓
Large cluster formation
↓
Cluster splits into smaller colonies
↓
Population decline
↓
Extinction

This behavior repeated multiple times throughout the experiment.

---

## 3. Green Species Persistence

Although many species appeared and disappeared:

- Green organisms remained present throughout most of the experiment.
- Green populations fluctuated but rarely disappeared.
- Green organisms repeatedly recovered after declines.

This suggests that the green lineage possesses traits that remain effective under food scarcity.

---

## 4. Population Stabilization at Lower Levels

Population generally remained around:

40–50 organisms

Examples observed:

Population = 40
Population = 50
Population = 51

Compared to previous experiments, overall population was significantly lower.

---

Observed average speeds:

~1.50
~1.52
~1.65

~2.2–2.5

A noticeable reduction in movement speed occurred.

This suggests that slower organisms were favored when food became scarce.

## 6. Clustering Continued Despite Scarcity

Even with reduced food:

- Organisms continued forming colonies.
- Multiple clusters appeared simultaneously.
- Territorial regions remained visible.

Examples observed:

2 massive green clusters
4–5 medium clusters
Several smaller colonies

Therefore clustering behavior did not disappear when food availability decreased.

---

# Emergent Behaviors

The following behaviors were not explicitly programmed but emerged naturally:

### Territorial Clustering

Organisms formed persistent colonies occupying specific regions of the world.

### Population Oscillation

Species repeatedly experienced:

Growth
↓
Expansion
↓
Collapse

without any direct code controlling these cycles.

### Competitive Species Replacement

Dominant species were frequently replaced by new mutant lineages.

### Evolution Toward Lower Speed

Food scarcity appeared to favor organisms with reduced movement speed and lower energy expenditure.

---

# Results

## Effects of Food Scarcity

Reducing food spawn rate from:

1.2 → 0.7

produced several significant changes:

- Lower population size
- Faster species turnover
- Reduced average movement speed
- Continued territorial clustering
- Increased extinction frequency
- Strong persistence of green lineages

---

# Conclusion

Food scarcity dramatically altered ecosystem dynamics.

The environment shifted from relatively stable dominance patterns toward frequent cycles of emergence, expansion, and extinction.

Despite reduced resources, clustering behavior remained present, indicating that territorial organization is an emergent property of the ecosystem rather than a direct consequence of food abundance.

A strong evolutionary trend toward slower movement speeds was observed, suggesting that energy-efficient organisms gain a survival advantage when resources are limited.

Green lineages consistently survived environmental pressure and repeatedly regained dominance, making them the most resilient species observed in this experiment.
