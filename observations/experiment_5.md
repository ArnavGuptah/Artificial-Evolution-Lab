# Experiment 5 Report

## Parameters
- Food Spawn Rate: 0.4 -> 0.7
- Mutation Rate: 0.18 -> 0.36

## Key Results
- Initial population boom (58 -> 100 by tick 600)
- Average speed peaked near 4.25, then steadily declined
- Long-term average speed stabilized around 1.5-1.7
- Population stabilized around 150-180 organisms
- Red species went extinct
- Green species became dominant
- Blue species survived as a secondary lineage
- Yellow and other minor species persisted in small numbers

## Emergent Behaviors
### Territorial Clustering
Green organisms formed persistent local colonies without explicit colony logic.

### Resource Partitioning
Green and blue lineages occupied different regions and behavioral niches.

### Stable Coexistence
Multiple species survived for tens of thousands of ticks instead of one lineage taking over.

### Large Organism Persistence
Large green organisms survived alongside smaller clustered descendants.

## Conclusion
High food availability combined with high mutation promoted biodiversity,
niche formation, clustering, and long-term ecosystem stability.

## Emergent Behaviors

### Stable High-Mutation Ecosystem

Mutation rate doubled.

Expected outcome:

- Chaos
- Frequent extinctions
- Instability

Observed outcome:

- Stable population
- Persistent colonies
- Species coexistence

---

### Red Lineage Extinction

Red species gradually disappeared and did not recover.

This represents the clearest lineage extinction observed so far.

---

### Green Dominance

Green again became dominant despite increased mutation pressure.

This strengthens the hypothesis that green occupies a robust evolutionary niche.

---

## Interpretation

High mutation increased diversity but did not destabilize the ecosystem.

Natural selection remained strong enough to filter unsuccessful mutations.

---

## Retrospective Notes

Experiment 5 demonstrated that:

- Mutation alone does not necessarily create chaos
- Stable ecosystems can persist under substantial genetic variation
- Evolution repeatedly converges toward slower, energy-efficient organisms
